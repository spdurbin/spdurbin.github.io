Didot vs. Bodoni

History of each typeface

Didot

Developed by Firmin Didot from 1784–1811, described as a Neoclassical serif typeface. It became the standard in French printing as Firmin Didot’s family owned the most influential and successful print shop and font foundry in France. The font was soon sought after by royalty and associated with luxury and grandeur. Firmin Didot was inspired by the Baskerville font with pronounced serifs and rounded letters. Didot is characterized by increased stroke contrast, hairline strokes, vertical stress, and unbracketed serifs.

Bodoni

Bodoni was designed by Giambattista Bodoni in the late 18th century, pushing a modern style rather than the traditional calligraphy that was commonly seen during this time. Giambattista Bodoni was the director of the Royal Typography in Parma, which is where the development of the typeface began. Inspired by the work of John Baskerville and French styles, this typeface is similar to Didot. The font is characterized by its thick and thin contrasts, unbracketed serifs, vertical stress, and small aperture. Bodoni was first released as a classical design, but it transitioned into being named a modern serif font. Because of the many renditions that have been done of Bodoni, the newer designs are now considered transitional rather than modern.

Comparison

Similarities

  • Both serif
  • Both radical thick and thins
  • Vertical stress
  • Unbracketed serifs

Differences

  • Didot’s O has a larger vertical stress.
  • Didot’s G has a spur at the chin, whereas Bodoni does not
  • Didot’s Q is more horizontal with a straight tail, whereas Bodoni's tail has more of a curve
  • Didot’s lowercase A top of counter has a more swoop/curve, whereas Bodoni is more straight
  • Didot has tabular lining figures
  • Bodoni’s lowercase r’s top is closer to stem than Didot
  • Bodoni has tabular oldstyle figures
